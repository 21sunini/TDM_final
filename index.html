Birth of a Fan

Exploring parasocial attachment through music, listening, and emotional intimacy

Research Question

This project explores how fandom forms through listening. Specifically, I am interested in parasocial attachment—the process by which emotional intimacy develops between a listener and a singer-songwriter through repeated engagement with music, media, and interpretation. Rather than treating fandom as instant devotion or obsession, I ask a quieter question: how does someone move from first encountering an artist's work to feeling a sense of familiarity, recognition, and connection that begins to resemble a relationship? In other words, how is a fan born?

Theoretical Framework

My thinking is grounded in Donald Horton and R. Richard Wohl's concept of parasocial interaction, which they describe as the "illusion of face-to-face relationship with the performer." These relationships are inherently one-sided, yet they feel meaningful because performers present consistent personas that listeners come to know over time. What interested me most was not whether this intimacy is "real" or "illusory," but how it is lived: how familiarity accumulates, how emotion attaches, and how listeners come to feel that an artist understands them—or helps them understand themselves.

Methodology

To explore this, I designed the project in two parts. In Voice of the Artist, I document my own journey into Olivia Dean's music through close listening, watching performances, and engaging with interviews and visuals. I tracked not just what I consumed, but how my feelings shifted as familiarity grew. In Voice of the Fans, I widen the frame, collecting audio fragments from interviews with fans to hear how others describe their own paths into connection, intimacy, and belonging. Together, these sections form a small archive of how parasocial attachment is experienced both privately and collectively.

Choosing the Artist

Stevie Nicks

John Mayer

Olivia Dean

Choosing an artist for this project made one thing immediately clear: fandom is not only about music, but about identity. I began with three options: Stevie Nicks, John Mayer, and Olivia Dean. Nicks was a rock legend I had always wanted to get into, I remembered liking an album from Mayer in high school, and a friend had informed me that Dean was up and coming. I listened to two albums from each and although I really wanted to like Nicks more than I did, I just wasn't feeling it. Mayer's two albums were great, but I was feeling mild hesitance as I considered some of his public exploits I had disliked in the past.

As I reasoned over these artists, I came to my first conclusion: Fandom isn't purely about the music. It's also about the singer-songwriter as a person because who you are friends with (or at least "feel" like you are friends with) says something about you, your interests, and your values. Horton and Wohl note that fans often feel that association with a performer transfers some of that persona's meaning onto themselves. I wanted to love Stevie Nicks because liking her would make me feel cool by association; I would borrow some of her rock-and-roll street cred to legitimate my own coolness. Similarly with Mayer, in becoming a fan and thereby associating with him, I felt like some of his poor reputation with Taylor Swift and her fans would rub off on me.

Olivia Dean ultimately felt different. As a young Black singer-songwriter close to my own age, she offered the possibility of discovering an artist in real time, without preconceived narratives or inherited fandom. Her music's openness, emotional honesty, and attention to many forms of love created space for connection to develop gradually. This project traces that development: how listening becomes attachment, how intimacy is felt through sound and voice, and how fandom emerges not all at once, but through attention, repetition, and care.

Voice of the Artist

This project begins with my own listening. Before turning outward to other fans, I wanted to understand what it feels like to move from casual exposure to deeper attachment: to sit with Olivia Dean's music, watch her performances, and gradually come to know her as an artist. In this phase, I listened closely to songs on repeat, to lyrics that followed me throughout the day, and to interviews and visuals that shaped how I understood who she is.

The Art of Loving

2025

  • The Art of Loving
    Feels soft and delicate, the way love itself does when it's new or imagined. Makes me wistful, like I'm stepping into a perfect cinematic moment, my own quiet Disney princess scene. Sets the emotional tone for the album: romantic, hopeful, and almost unreal.
  • Nice to Each Other
    Captures the awkward, tender back-and-forth of falling in love before anyone wants to name it. I love the way the energy picks up midway, mirroring the excitement and hesitation of being on the edge of something. What if instead of defining things, we just try being nice to each other?
  • Lady Lady
    Makes me cry. It feels like a reflection on growing up and slowly coming to know myself, especially as I stand on the edge of graduation. It makes me excited for who I'm becoming, and I like to think my younger self would be excited too.
  • Close Up
    Taps into my fear of being truly seen. What if you when you get to know me you don't like me anymore? Strong feelings of putting myself out there and wondering if it was a mistake, definitely hits deeply.
  • So Easy to Fall in Love
    This is what a love story in Paris sounds like. Light, breezy, sweet, and romantic, and it captures how I want to be seen by someone else. It makes falling in love feel effortless and beautiful, I'm a Disney princess again for the length of the song.
  • Let Alone the One You Love
    Good for crying. Really sits with the pain of betrayal and heartbreak, especially when it comes from someone who should have cared most. Who would do that to a friend, let alone the one you love?
  • Man I Need
    My very first Olivia Dean song! She is speaking directly to my heart: wanting love, but also wanting someone to step up and meet you where you are. Upbeat and fun, but also grown, vulnerable, and honest.
  • Something in Between
    Didn't stand out to me emotionally in the same way as others.
  • Loud
    Simple but powerful guitar and melody. Controlled drama and restrained anguish that really draws me in. Her style here is emotional and expressive, reminds me a lot of RAYE.
  • Baby Steps
    Hopeful and grounding. It reminds me that it's okay to pick yourself up slowly and keep going. Strength and softness can coexist, growth doesn't have to be rushed.
  • A Couple Minutes
    Everything I love: slow, sweet, soft, a little sexy, a little sad. Feels deeply personal and intimate. Love's never wasted when it's shared.
  • I've Seen It
    Didn't leave a strong impression on me emotionally.

Album Reflection

Overall, this album feels like a portrait of being in your twenties: messy, hopeful, tender, and uncertain. It captures the simultaneous excitement of becoming someone new and the sadness of leaving parts of yourself behind. Listening to it, I feel seen in my desire to grow, to love deeply, and to be loved in return.

Messy

2023

  • UFO
    Feels like drifting until someone gently pulls you back to Earth. There's a quiet comfort in the idea of being anchored by another person when you feel unmoored.
  • Dive
    So bright, upbeat, and playful; it makes falling in love feel fun and impulsive like choosing to dive in without overthinking it. Reminds me of early 2010s romcoms where everything feels possible and a little reckless.
  • Ladies Room
    Groovy and confident, perfectly matching the song's self-love energy. I love the title too, it feels like a subtle nod to A Room of One's Own, a private space for women to claim themselves. Unapologetic, grounded, and a little Michael Jackson-coded at the end.
  • No Man
    Powerful and dangerous, like something a siren might sing. There's a scorned, femme fatale energy and it has almost operatic intensity even though she's lamenting.
  • Dangerously Easy
    Bittersweet in a way that hurts. Captures the feeling of watching someone move on effortlessly while you're still stuck in the mess of it all. What have I got to do to make it look easy, dangerously easy, like you?
  • Getting There – Interlude
    Beautiful, rich transition music.
  • Danger
    Catchy and addictive sound that won't leave my head, mirroring the awareness of danger, but also inability to resist loving someone anyway. Perfectly captures the feeling of being scared and pulled forward at the same time.
  • The Hardest Part
    Feels like the moment after the third-act breakup in a romcom, when the main character finally chooses herself. Realizing you've grown and changed, even if someone else wants you to stay the same.
  • I Could Be a Florist
    Heartbreakingly soft and vulnerable. It's hopeful and melancholic and longing. There's something so deeply tender about daydreams, they feel so human and fragile. Lingered in my heart.
  • Messy
    Feels like reassurance. You don't need to be perfect or prepared. It's okay to be messy, someone will still stand beside you. Steady, comforting, powerful.
  • Everybody's Crazy
    Caught me off guard. It's raw and exposed and the line about squeezing someone's hand under the table made me cry unexpectedly; it's such a small, sad image, but it carries so much need. Reminded me of Joni Mitchell in its honesty and emotional clarity.
  • Carmen
    Feels like a tribute and a thank-you. Honors her grandmother and the legacy she carries forward, and including her voice makes it even more intimate. Cue even more tears.

Album Reflection

Messy unfolded slowly for me, but once it did, it hit deeply. The vulnerability in the lyrics—naming anxieties, fears, hopes, and desires I recognize in myself—touched something I wasn't expecting. I found myself crying without fully understanding why, the same way I did the first time I listened to Billie Eilish and experienced the shock of hearing someone mirror your inner world so clearly. This album made me feel less alone in wanting, fearing, and dreaming the way I do.

Man I Need

Live Performance

Seeing this song performed live changes it completely. Her facial expressions, dancing, and huge smile give the lyrics a cheeky playfulness and confidence that you don't experience as fully when just listening. Watching how she gets lost in the performance reminds me of singing alone in my room. Her energy is infectious, and it makes the desire in the song feel not just hopeful, but genuine.

Messy

Live Film

This film is deeply intimate in its simplicity. There's something powerful about hearing the most emotional songs on the album for me personally (Everybody's Crazy, Messy, Carmen) in this setting: outdoors, handheld camera, simple outfits, her voice asking to be understood while surrounded by open space. It makes her anxiety and vulnerability feel grounded and human. We're all just trying to figure ourselves out in a big, overwhelming world and watching her perform alongside her band in the field deepens that feeling of connection; the joy between the musicians feels genuine and present. Carmen was especially moving here. Singing about her grandmother's legacy while standing in nature makes the grief feel expansive rather than closed-off, like she want to share her memory with the whole world. As the film ends with her dancing into the darkness, backed by full, swelling sound, it feels like a quiet declaration of moving forward and carrying love, loss, and memory with her. Seeing these songs performed this way solidified what I already felt listening to the album: that the honesty of the music comes from humility. The simplicity of the setting lets the emotion lead, making the music feel lived-in rather than performed.

Album Interview

Interview

I love how comfortable and unguarded she comes across. Even small details like her wearing the tinted glasses so casually suggest genuine ease rather than performance. Her discussion about not "hearing herself" in her earlier music, and the need to retreat home to reconnect also deeply resonated; I know that feeling of needing quiet and solitude to find your own voice again, especially in today's digital age. Learning that The Art of Loving was inspired by bell hooks made everything click. Her expansive view of love (romantic, platonic, self-directed) feels intentional, not accidental. When she described wanting the album to feel warm, like a heated blanket you can return to, I realized that perfectly matched my listening experience. She comes across as thoughtful, silly, and sincere—a great conversationalist who values honesty, growth, and care, both in her music and in love.

Vanity Fair

Interview

This interview made her feel strikingly human. I loved her moment of self-acceptance when she named what she likes about her appearance without hesitation. It felt like a quiet but powerful example of self-love. Hearing her inner dialogue out loud also reminded me of my own: the constant toggling between confidence and self-consciousness, wanting to be seen and wanting to disappear. I also like how she talks about creativity as something playful rather than precious, emphasizing process over product, definitely a refreshing perspective. Her stories about family, music, independence, and wandering anonymously through the city made her feel deeply relatable, like someone in her twenties figuring things out in real time (just like me!). The final sculpture she created didn't look like her, but "sparked joy," and that captures her spirit perfectly.

Apple Music

Interview

My admiration for her only grew after this interview. She jokes that fear kept her from certain milestones, but her admission was incredibly honest. Fear doesn't vanish just because success arrives. I was also struck by her unwavering commitment to truth in her music: she won't write anything she hasn't lived, knowing she has to carry those songs for years. Learning about her background (The BRIT School, her long-term band collaboration, starting by busking and playing tiny gigs) gives her success a real foundation but also explains her grounded demeanor and familiar sound (Raye, Adele, Winehouse, and many others whom I think she sounds like also attended The BRIT). When she explained that she avoids album features to hear and protect her own voice, I again felt deeply understood. She leaves me with the impression of someone proud, healed, grateful, and entirely herself: ambitious, playful, and fully present in the life she's created.

Reflections

Reflecting on this process, I can see how my relationship with Olivia Dean's music has shifted. The songs function not as simple sounds, but as emotional reference points: tools for navigating my moods, relationships, and self. Through repeated listening, performances, and interviews, an image of Dean has began to form that familiar but not known, intimately felt but not personal. This section highlights the beginning of fandom through private, quiet acts of listening, an experience many share. Next, I shift focus to how others articulate this very same connection.

Voice of the Fans

After tracing my own journey into Olivia Dean's music, I wanted to understand how fandom feels beyond myself. In this phase, I spoke with fans to understand how their relationships with her music began, deepened, and took shape over time. Rather than presenting full interviews, I've collected audio snippets of moments where fans describe recognition, aspiration, vulnerability, and belonging.

Listening across these voices reveals that fandom does not follow a single path. Some fans relate to Olivia Dean as a future version of themselves; others experience her music as a guide that reshapes how they think and feel. Together, these clips form a small archive of how parasocial intimacy, authenticity, and connection are lived—not just by one listener, but across a community.

Closing Thoughts

Listening across these fan voices, it becomes clear that fandom does not begin or end at the same place for everyone. Some fans find closeness through aspiration, others through reflection or shared identity. Some value distance as authenticity, while others feel intimacy deepen through visibility. What unites these experiences is not intensity, but attention: how music is returned to again and again, used to name emotions, imagine futures, and feel less alone. In these fragments, fandom appears less as a label and more as a relationship with the artist, shaped over time, mediated by sound, and sustained through listening.

Reflections

Understanding parasocial attachment, authenticity, and the practice of fandom

The Process of Becoming

This project began with a set of questions that felt deceptively simple: when does a casual listener become a fan? How does an artist's work come to feel personal enough to shape identity? And why does that intimacy feel real, even when the relationship is one-sided? By following fandom as a process, first through my own listening, then through the voices of other fans, I came to see that these questions cannot be answered by pointing to a single moment, platform, or behavior. Instead, fandom emerges through accumulation: of attention, interpretation, and emotional investment over time.

Intimacy as Practice

What this project ultimately reveals is that parasocial attachment is less about illusion and more about practice. Intimacy is not created simply because an artist shares personal details, nor because fans believe they "know" them in a literal sense. Rather, intimacy forms through repeated acts of listening and return—through songs that are revisited in different emotional contexts, performances that give bodies and gestures to sound, and interviews that provide just enough detail to make an artist feel relatable without ever being fully known. The relationship feels real not because it is reciprocal, but because it is useful: it helps listeners make sense of their feelings, aspirations, and transitions.

Authenticity as Negotiation

Across fan voices, authenticity also emerges as something negotiated rather than inherent. Some fans experience authenticity through restraint and distance, others through vulnerability and expression. What matters is not how much of the artist is revealed, but whether what is revealed aligns with how the music feels. Authenticity is co-produced by listeners who decide what feels trustworthy, grounded, or sincere. This helps explain why fandom can feel so personal and yet vary so widely: fans are not consuming the same relationship, even when they are consuming the same artist.

Fandom as Identity Work

Perhaps most importantly, this project reframes fandom not as obsession or escapism, but as a site of identity work. Fans use music to name emotions they cannot articulate, to imagine future versions of themselves, and to locate belonging, sometimes alone, sometimes within a community. In this way, fandom functions less like devotion to a person and more like participation in a shared emotional language. Being a fan becomes a way of saying something about who you are, what you value, and how you move through the world.

Parasocial relationships matter because they shape how people understand intimacy itself. In a world where connection is increasingly mediated, fandom offers a model of closeness that does not rely on reciprocity, but still feels meaningful, grounding, and real. To take fandom seriously is to take seriously the ways people learn to feel, care, and belong—often first through listening.
